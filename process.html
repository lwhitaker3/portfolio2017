My Process

I believe the most successful innovations are the ones that consider the end user during each stage of development. The reality is, no matter how revolutionary a product is, it canâ€™t succeed if no one can use it. I am passionate about developing experiences that are intuitive and make technology more accessible to everyone.

Step 1

Background Research

Before diving into design it's important to gather all of the necessary information. To me this includes everything from the project requirements to what products are already on the market to what your target users are looking for.

Step 2

Brainstorm/Sketching

For me brainstorming is about getting all of my ideas onto paper or a whiteboard, or even post-its. It can help me get past ideas that might be stuck in my head to get to the better ideas. I think two heads is better than one is definitely true when coming up with ideas. I like to be able to sit with people with different roles like developers, graphic diesigners and so on because its nice to hear different perpectives. It's also nice to be able to throw ideas out and see if as a group you are able to build of them and come up with something even better.

Step 3

Wireframing/Prototyping

I start the design process by looking at the main tasks a user should be able to accomplish with the product and create user flows for them. I develop site maps to organize the content. Then I create low-fi wireframes to work through how the interactions will work to complete the main tasks. I try to get a prototype working as early on in the design process as I can because prototypes show the problem with the system very quickly and it allows me to be able to move on to testing.

Step 4

User Testing

Testing a product with users is invaluable. My main job is to advocate for the user, but I can't do that effectively if I don't get the product infront of them and find out what works and doesn't work from their perspective.

Step 5

Iterate

As I get feedback on the product I go back to the drawing board, redesign and retest the product. Iterative design processes give us better products because its impossible to predict all the issues a product will run into. Be setting up an iterative work flow you can adapt and be much more flexible.
